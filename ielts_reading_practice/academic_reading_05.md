# IELTS Academic Reading Practice 05 (Band 7.5-8.0, Mixed Question Types)

## Passage: When Algorithms Meet Public Decisions

### A
Over the last decade, public agencies have adopted algorithmic tools to support decisions in areas such as welfare eligibility, school admissions, tax auditing, and policing. Advocates argue that these systems improve consistency by reducing human variability. Critics counter that consistency is not the same as fairness, especially when models are trained on historical data shaped by past inequalities. The policy debate has therefore shifted from whether algorithms are used to how their use is governed.

### B
One recurring misconception is that algorithmic decision support is objective simply because it is computational. In reality, every model embeds human choices: which variables are included, how outcomes are defined, and what error trade-offs are tolerated. These design choices are often invisible to the public, yet they determine who is classified as high risk, low priority, or ineligible. Technical opacity can make contested decisions appear neutral even when value judgments are deeply involved.

### C
Performance metrics further complicate evaluation. A model can achieve high overall accuracy while still producing severe disparities across subgroups. For example, false-positive rates may be concentrated in communities that are already heavily monitored. Policymakers who focus on aggregate performance may therefore overlook distributional harm. This is why many researchers recommend reporting disaggregated results rather than single headline scores.

### D
Proponents sometimes suggest that algorithms merely assist, while final authority remains with human officials. However, behavioral evidence indicates that recommendation systems can create automation bias: users may over-trust machine outputs, especially under time pressure. In high-volume administrative settings, staff often lack the capacity to independently review each case. Human oversight then exists formally but weakens in practice.

### E
Data governance is another critical layer. Public-sector models frequently integrate records collected for different purposes and under different legal regimes. Merging such datasets can improve predictive power, but it also increases privacy risk and the potential for function creep, where data gathered for one purpose is gradually reused for another. Without strict purpose limitation and audit trails, these expansions can occur incrementally and with limited public awareness.

### F
Some governments have responded with transparency measures, including algorithm registers, impact assessments, and appeal channels. These tools can improve accountability, but their effectiveness depends on depth rather than symbolism. A register that lists model names without documentation of data sources, validation methods, or error patterns offers little practical scrutiny. Likewise, appeal rights are weaker if affected individuals cannot understand how decisions were reached.

### G
Regulation also faces a pacing problem. Model updates can occur monthly or even continuously, while legal review and procurement cycles move far more slowly. A system approved under one configuration may drift into materially different behavior over time. Static certification is therefore insufficient in dynamic environments. Several experts now advocate continuous monitoring frameworks with trigger thresholds for mandatory re-evaluation.

### H
The central lesson is not that algorithmic tools should be banned from public administration. Rather, legitimacy depends on institutional design: clear accountability for outcomes, measurable fairness standards, meaningful contestability, and ongoing oversight after deployment. Where these safeguards are absent, efficiency gains can come at the cost of trust. Where they are robust, computational systems may support better decisions without displacing democratic responsibility.

## Quick Core Vocabulary

- `disaggregated` broken down by subgroup
- `automation bias` over-trusting automated recommendations
- `function creep` expanding reuse beyond original purpose
- `purpose limitation` legal restriction on data reuse scope
- `contestability` ability to challenge and review decisions
