# IELTS Academic Reading Practice 06 (Band 7.5-8.0, Exam-Style)

## Passage: Can Urban Cooling Be Designed Without Air Conditioning?

### A
As heatwaves become more frequent, many cities are searching for cooling strategies that do not rely exclusively on air conditioning. Mechanical cooling has saved lives, but it also increases electricity demand, raises peak-load risk, and can worsen outdoor heat through waste heat discharge. Urban planners therefore face a difficult task: reducing heat exposure while avoiding solutions that intensify long-term energy stress.

### B
One widely discussed approach is passive urban cooling through material and form. High-albedo surfaces reflect more solar radiation, while permeable and vegetated areas reduce heat storage. Street geometry also matters. Narrow canyons with poor ventilation can trap heat, whereas carefully oriented corridors may improve airflow and nighttime cooling. However, these effects vary by climate zone, and design rules that work in dry regions may underperform in humid ones.

### C
Tree cover is often presented as a universal answer, yet evidence is more conditional. Canopy shade can significantly lower surface temperatures and improve thermal comfort for pedestrians. At the same time, dense planting in low-wind corridors may reduce air movement, and poorly selected species can increase water demand in drought-prone areas. The strongest outcomes are usually observed when greening is combined with species planning, irrigation strategy, and maintenance capacity.

### D
Building design introduces another layer. External shading, insulation, and cross-ventilation can reduce indoor heat gain before cooling equipment is needed. In many retrofit projects, these interventions deliver larger energy savings than expected because they operate continuously rather than only during extreme events. Yet adoption remains uneven: upfront costs, split incentives between landlords and tenants, and limited technical expertise all slow implementation.

### E
Public-health researchers emphasize that heat risk is socially uneven. Elderly residents, outdoor workers, and low-income households often experience the highest exposure and have the least adaptive capacity. As a result, citywide average temperature reduction is not sufficient as a policy target. Distribution matters. A modest intervention in high-vulnerability districts may produce greater health benefit than a larger intervention in already well-served zones.

### F
Monitoring systems are improving heat governance. Satellite observations, street-level sensors, and mobile measurements can now map microclimate variation at fine resolution. These datasets help identify hotspots that are invisible in coarse city averages. But measurement alone does not guarantee action. If planning departments lack authority or funding to respond, high-quality maps may document inequality without reducing it.

### G
Some policymakers promote integrated heat-action plans that combine infrastructure, emergency response, labor protections, and communication protocols. Critics warn that such plans can become symbolic if performance metrics are vague. For example, counting cooling centers says little about accessibility, opening hours, or public awareness. Effective governance requires indicators tied to real usage and health outcomes, not just visible outputs.

### H
The emerging consensus is pragmatic rather than ideological. Air conditioning will remain necessary in many contexts, especially during extreme events. But long-term resilience depends on reducing baseline heat exposure through urban design, social targeting, and institutional follow-through. In this framework, the key question is not whether cities should choose one tool, but whether they can coordinate multiple tools at the right scale and in the right places.

## Quick Core Vocabulary

- `high-albedo` highly reflective of solar radiation
- `thermal comfort` how comfortable people feel in heat conditions
- `split incentives` costs and benefits borne by different parties
- `microclimate` local atmospheric conditions in a small area
- `baseline exposure` normal day-to-day level of risk before extremes
